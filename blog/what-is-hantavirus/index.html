What is Hantavirus?

Hantavirus is a family of viruses carried by rodents — primarily rats and mice — that can cause severe and life-threatening illness in humans. While the virus is not new, it has surged into global headlines in May 2026 following a deadly outbreak aboard a cruise ship in the Atlantic Ocean, prompting the World Health Organization to issue urgent guidance.

The name "hantavirus" comes from the Hantan River in South Korea, where the virus was first identified in the 1950s during the Korean War. It is classified in the family Hantaviridae and infects humans primarily through contact with infected rodent urine, droppings, or saliva.

ℹ️

Key Fact

Hantavirus is not a new disease. The WHO classified it in 1987. However, the 2026 cruise ship outbreak is the first major international cluster in years, putting it back in the spotlight.

History & Discovery of Hantavirus

During the Korean War (1950–1953), thousands of United Nations soldiers developed a mysterious illness causing fever, kidney failure, and hemorrhaging. Researchers later traced it to a rodent-borne virus, eventually named Hantaan virus after the river near which it was first isolated.

The virus gained broader global attention in 1993 when a cluster of previously healthy young adults died from unexplained severe respiratory illness in the "Four Corners" region of the United States (where Arizona, New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado meet). This outbreak introduced the world to Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS) — a deadly lung disease caused by the Sin Nombre hantavirus, carried by deer mice.

Most recently, hantavirus made news in the United States in 2025 when an autopsy confirmed that Betsy Arakawa, wife of renowned actor Gene Hackman, had died from the virus. Then in 2026, a new cluster on a cruise ship reignited global concern.

Types of Hantavirus — Old World vs. New World

Hantaviruses are broadly divided into two categories based on geography and the disease they cause:

Old World Hantaviruses (Europe & Asia)

These strains cause Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome (HFRS), which primarily attacks the kidneys and blood vessels. Examples include Hantaan virus, Seoul virus, Puumala virus, and Dobrava virus. Fatality rates for Old World strains range from less than 1% (Puumala) to 5–15% (Hantaan/Dobrava).

New World Hantaviruses (The Americas)

These strains — found in North, Central, and South America — cause Hantavirus Cardiopulmonary Syndrome (HCPS), also called Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS). These are far more deadly, with fatality rates reaching up to 50% in some cases.

The Andes Virus — The Most Dangerous Strain

The Andes virus, found in Argentina and Chile, is the only known hantavirus strain that has documented human-to-human transmission. It is the strain linked to the 2026 cruise ship outbreak and was responsible for the deadly 2018 Epuyen outbreak in Argentina that killed 11 people. The WHO has classified hantaviruses, including Andes virus, as emerging priority pathogens with high pandemic potential.

How Does Hantavirus Spread? (Transmission)

Understanding transmission is critical to preventing infection. Hantavirus is primarily a zoonotic disease — meaning it passes from animals to humans, not easily from person to person.

Primary Route: Rodent Droppings & Aerosols

The most common way humans get infected is by inhaling aerosolized particles from infected rodent urine, droppings, or saliva. This happens when contaminated materials are disturbed in enclosed or poorly ventilated spaces — like cleaning an old shed, barn, cabin, or storage room.

"Rats jump on ships or are carried in cargo, they poop and pee, air currents aerosolize it, carry it, and it can get in the lungs, causing serious pulmonary illness."

— Dr. John Lednicky, Research Professor, University of Florida Emerging Pathogens Institute

Other Routes of Transmission

  • Direct contact: Touching rodent droppings, urine, or saliva and then touching your eyes, nose, or mouth
  • Rodent bites: Rare, but possible
  • Entry through cuts or wounds: If contaminated material contacts broken skin

Human-to-Human Transmission

This is where the Andes virus differs dangerously from other strains. While the CDC and WHO confirm that most hantaviruses do NOT spread between humans, the Andes virus has shown limited human-to-human transmission in previous outbreaks, particularly among household members or intimate partners during the early fever phase.

🚫

Common Misconception: "It Spreads Like Flu"

Hantavirus is NOT airborne like influenza. You cannot catch it by being in the same room as an infected person in most cases. You must inhale virus-carrying particles released when rodent-contaminated materials are disturbed. The virus also only survives on surfaces for a few days.

Hantavirus Symptoms — A Stage-by-Stage Guide

Symptoms typically appear 1 to 8 weeks after exposure (2–4 weeks is most common), though some cases have reported onset up to 40 days after exposure. The disease progresses in distinct phases:

Phase Timing Symptoms
🟡 Early (Prodromal) Days 1–5 Fever, fatigue, muscle aches (especially thighs, hips, back), headache, chills, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach pain
🔴 Late / Cardiopulmonary (HPS) Days 4–10 Shortness of breath, coughing, chest tightness, lungs fill with fluid (pulmonary edema), rapid heart rate, drop in blood pressure
🔴 Renal Phase (HFRS) Days 5–14 Low blood pressure, internal bleeding, reduced urine output, acute kidney failure (Old World strains)
🟢 Recovery Weeks–Months Gradual improvement; full recovery can take weeks to months; some patients develop long-term complications
🆘

Warning Signs — Seek Emergency Care Immediately

  • Sudden severe shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
  • Persistent high fever following flu-like illness
  • Extreme fatigue that rapidly worsens
  • Low blood pressure / feeling faint
  • Known recent exposure to rodents or their droppings

How is Hantavirus Diagnosed?

Diagnosing hantavirus is challenging because early symptoms closely resemble influenza and other common illnesses. Doctors typically consider the diagnosis when a patient presents with severe respiratory illness and a history of potential rodent exposure.

Diagnostic methods include:

  • PCR Testing (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Detects viral genetic material — the gold standard for confirmation
  • Serological Testing: Blood tests detecting antibodies (IgM/IgG) against hantavirus
  • Immunofluorescence Assay: Used in specialized labs
  • Metagenomics Sequencing: Advanced genomic analysis used in outbreak investigations

Importantly, diagnosing hantavirus in someone infected less than 72 hours ago is very difficult. If initial tests are negative, repeat testing is usually done 72 hours after symptom onset.

Hantavirus Treatment — Is There a Cure?

As of 2026, there is no approved antiviral treatment or vaccine for hantavirus infection. This makes prevention and early supportive care critically important.

Current Treatment Approach

  • Hospitalization: Patients with severe symptoms require immediate hospital admission
  • Supportive care: Rest, hydration, oxygen support
  • Mechanical ventilation / intubation: For patients with severe respiratory failure
  • ECMO (Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation): Advanced life support that has shown promise in reducing mortality in severe HPS cases
  • ICU monitoring: Close observation of cardiac, respiratory, and kidney function
💡

Early Treatment Saves Lives

The WHO emphasizes that early access to intensive care significantly improves outcomes, especially for hantavirus cardiopulmonary syndrome. Patients who reach an ICU early have substantially better survival odds than those who seek care late.

The 2026 Hantavirus Cruise Ship Outbreak: What We Know

In early May 2026, the world's attention turned to a Dutch cruise ship, MV Hondius, after passengers began falling severely ill with respiratory illness. The ship had been travelling from Ushuaia, Argentina to Cape Verde, off the west coast of Africa.

Key Timeline of Events

  • April 1, 2026: MV Hondius departs from Argentina with 147 passengers and crew
  • April 6–28: Illness onset in multiple passengers — fever, gastrointestinal symptoms, rapid deterioration to pneumonia
  • April 24–26: Two passengers evacuated to Johannesburg, South Africa; one dies upon arrival
  • April 26–28: Additional passengers fall ill; one more death reported
  • May 2, 2026: WHO is notified of the cluster; PCR testing confirms hantavirus infection in the first case
  • May 3, 2026: Ship arrives in waters off Cape Verde; remains stationary
  • May 4, 2026: WHO reports 7 cases (2 confirmed, 5 suspected), 3 deaths, 1 critically ill in ICU
  • May 5–7, 2026: International response coordinated across Cabo Verde, Netherlands, Spain, South Africa, and the UK
🌍

Which Strain? The Andes Virus

Investigators believe the cases are linked to the Andes virus, the same strain responsible for the 2018 Epuyen, Argentina outbreak. Cases 1 and 2 had travelled through Argentina before boarding. Because Andes virus has a history of human-to-human transmission, the WHO initiated contact tracing for all passengers and crew, as well as for individuals on the same evacuation flights.

"We do know that some of the cases had very close contact with each other and certainly human-to-human transmission can't be ruled out — so as a precaution, this is what we are assuming."

— Dr. Maria Van Kerkhove, WHO Director for Epidemic and Pandemic Preparedness

How to Protect Yourself from Hantavirus

Since there is no vaccine or cure, prevention is the single most important tool. The majority of these steps centre on reducing contact with rodents and their waste.

At Home

  • Seal all holes, gaps, and cracks in walls, floors, and around pipes to prevent rodents from entering
  • Store food (including pet food) in airtight, rodent-proof containers
  • Keep garbage in thick containers with tight-fitting lids
  • Use snap traps (not poison bait) to control rodent populations — poison bait causes rodents to die in hidden spots where they can be disturbed later
  • Keep your home clutter-free, especially in storage areas where rodents hide

Cleaning Rodent-Infested Areas (Critical Safety Steps)

  • NEVER sweep or vacuum rodent droppings — this aerosolizes the virus
  • Wear rubber or plastic gloves before cleaning any contaminated area
  • Spray droppings, urine, and nesting materials with a disinfectant or bleach solution (1 part bleach to 10 parts water) and let sit for 5 minutes
  • Wipe up using paper towels, then double-bag and seal all waste before disposal
  • Wash hands thoroughly after removing gloves
  • In heavily infested areas, wear an N95 respirator mask

Outdoor & Travel Precautions

  • Do not camp or sleep in areas with visible signs of rodent activity
  • Store food off the ground when camping
  • Before using a cabin or holiday home after a period of vacancy, ventilate it for at least 30 minutes before entering
  • Avoid agricultural areas in South America where Andes virus is endemic if you are immunocompromised

Is Pakistan at Risk from Hantavirus?

This is a question many Pakistanis are understandably asking. Here is what the evidence tells us:

Pakistan is not in the primary endemic zones for the most dangerous hantavirus strains. The highly deadly Andes virus is found in South America. However, Seoul virus, an Old World hantavirus strain with a much lower fatality rate (under 1%), is found worldwide, including in Asia, and is associated with common brown rats (Rattus norvegicus).

Given Pakistan's urban density, issues with solid waste management, and the presence of rat populations in many cities and rural areas, there is always a theoretical baseline risk of Seoul virus exposure. That said, major hantavirus outbreaks have not been documented in Pakistan, and the WHO's current assessment is that the global risk from the 2026 cruise ship outbreak remains very low for the general public.

🇵🇰

Advice for Pakistani Residents

There is no need for panic. The 2026 outbreak is geographically linked to South America and the Atlantic. However, maintaining basic rodent control in homes is good year-round practice. If you develop severe, unexplained respiratory illness after potential rodent exposure, see a doctor immediately and mention the exposure history.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Is hantavirus contagious between humans?

For most hantavirus strains — no. The Andes virus is the only exception, and even then, transmission requires prolonged close contact, typically among household members, and appears most likely during the early fever phase.

Can hantavirus be treated with antibiotics?

No. Hantavirus is a virus, not a bacterial infection, so antibiotics are ineffective. There is currently no approved antiviral drug that targets hantavirus. Treatment is supportive care in a hospital setting.

How long does hantavirus survive outside a host?

The hantavirus is relatively fragile outside a host. It survives on surfaces for only a few days under normal conditions, and is destroyed by sunlight, disinfectants, and bleach solutions.

Can I catch hantavirus from my pet?

Pet cats and dogs are not known to carry or transmit hantavirus. However, pet rodents (rats, hamsters, gerbils) could theoretically carry Seoul virus, though this is very rare with properly bred domestic animals. Wild rodents remain the primary risk.

What is the difference between HPS and HFRS?

HPS (Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome) is caused by New World strains (Americas) and primarily attacks the lungs and heart. HFRS (Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome) is caused by Old World strains (Europe and Asia) and primarily attacks the kidneys. HPS has a significantly higher fatality rate.

Is there a hantavirus vaccine?

As of May 2026, there is no WHO-approved, commercially available hantavirus vaccine. Some experimental vaccines and therapeutics are being researched in labs, including at the University of Florida Emerging Pathogens Institute.
